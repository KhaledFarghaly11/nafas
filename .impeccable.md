# Nafas Design Context

## Users

**Customers**: Egyptians who want real homemade food — not restaurant delivery, but the taste of home. They browse during meals or when hungry, seeking comfort and authenticity. They trust the cook personally; this is a relationship, not a transaction.

**Chefs (Homemakers)**: Egyptian women (primarily) who cook from their own kitchens. They are proud of their food but may not be tech-savvy. They need clarity, simplicity, and respect in the interface. Their experience runs in dark mode (evening kitchen context).

## Brand Personality

**Warm, honest, homemade**

- **Warm**: Inviting, not corporate. Like walking into a friend's kitchen. Every surface feels lived-in and comfortable.
- **Honest**: No stock photos, no inflated promises. Real food, real cooks, real portions. The UI is straightforward — what you see is what you get.
- **Homemade**: This is not a restaurant. The design celebrates imperfection and personality. No sterile grids of identical cards; instead, variety and human touch.

### Emotional Goals

- **Primary**: Hungry and curious — the interface should make food look irresistible and encourage exploration. Appetite appeal is the #1 design priority.
- **Secondary**: Comforted and connected — feeling that this is trustworthy, personal, and rooted in real Egyptian food culture.

### Anti-References

- **NOT like Talabat/Uber Eats**: Avoid corporate, transactional, blue/white delivery app feel. No sterile grids, no stock food photography layouts, no "choose your restaurant" browsing patterns. Nafas is about people, not businesses.

## Aesthetic Direction

### Visual Tone

Warm, earthy, textural — like a well-used kitchen. The cream/clay/saffron palette creates an appetite-stimulating warmth. Typography blends the friendliness of Tajawal (Arabic display) with the refinement of Cormorant Garamond (prices/numerals). The overall effect should feel like browsing a handwritten menu on a kitchen wall.

### Theme Strategy (Already Implemented)

- **Customers**: Light theme (cream #FAF3E8 background) — daytime browsing, bright and appetizing
- **Chefs**: Dark theme (deep oud #1A110A background) — evening kitchen context, reduced eye strain

### Typography

- **Display (Arabic)**: Tajawal — friendly, rounded, approachable
- **Body (Arabic)**: Noto Sans Arabic — clean, highly legible at small sizes
- **Prices/Numerals**: Cormorant Garamond — elegant serif for prices, adding a touch of refinement
- **English**: Uses the same font families for consistency in bilingual contexts

### Color Philosophy

- Clay (#C1714F) is the brand — warm, earthy, appetizing. Use for CTAs and key interactions.
- Saffron (#E8A838) is the spice — accent color for highlights, badges, attention.
- Cream (#FAF3E8) is the tablecloth — the warm background that makes everything feel inviting.
- Mint (#7DADA0) and Rose (#D4927A) are seasonal garnishes — use sparingly for status and variety.

### What Makes This Unforgettable

The food-first, people-first approach. When someone opens Nafas, they shouldn't feel like they're using a delivery app — they should feel like they're peeking into a neighbor's kitchen. Personality and warmth in every detail, not just in the colors but in the micro-copy, the spacing, the way information is revealed.

## Design Principles

1. **Appetite First**: Every screen should make you hungrier. Food imagery, warm colors, and generous spacing let dishes breathe and look their best.

2. **People Over Businesses**: Always surface the cook behind the food. Names, faces, stories — this is what separates Nafas from Talabat.

3. **Honest Warmth**: No decorative UI that doesn't serve the user. No stock patterns. Every element should feel like it belongs in a kitchen, not a boardroom.

4. **Bilingual by Nature**: Arabic is the heart language. Design for Arabic-first, then verify English. RTL is not an afterthought — it's the primary layout direction.

5. **Two Worlds, One Brand**: Customer and chef experiences share the same soul but adapt to their context (light/dark, browse/manage, hungry/busy). The design system unifies them through shared tokens and components.

6. **Accessible by Design**: Inclusive experiences for all users — screen reader support via accessibilityRole/Label/State, WCAG 44px touch targets, reduced-motion animation, and proper heading semantics. Accessibility is built into primitives, not bolted on afterward.
